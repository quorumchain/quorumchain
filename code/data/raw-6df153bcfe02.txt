### V1 — INDETERMINATE
This question has no certifiable external answer, and the reason is structural, not merely empirical.

**The definitional trap.** The verdict turns on what "genuine reasoning" *means*, and there is no agreed operationalization that cleanly separates it from "sophisticated pattern-matching." Two failure modes:

- If "genuine reasoning" is defined behaviorally (produces valid multi-step inferences, generalizes to novel problems, corrects errors), current LLMs clearly do *some* of it — so the answer leans YES, but trivially, by construction.
- If "genuine reasoning" requires a particular *substrate or process* (grounded understanding, causal world-models, non-statistical inference), then "sophisticated pattern-matching" is defined to be the negation — so the answer is NO, also by construction.

The dichotomy in the question may itself be false. Pattern-matching at sufficient scale and abstraction is not obviously distinct from what we call reasoning in humans; the human brain is also, at some level, a predictive pattern-matcher. So "genuine reasoning *as opposed to* pattern-matching" presupposes a clean boundary that no one has certified.

**No external anchor.** There is no benchmark, experiment, or authority that settles this. Benchmark performance is contested (contamination, shortcut-learning); interpretability research shows genuine intermediate computation in some cases and shallow heuristics in others. The empirical picture is *mixed*, not decisive — and even a complete mechanistic account wouldn't resolve the *definitional* dispute about whether that mechanism "counts."

**The validator-is-an-LLM problem.** I am the very kind of system under judgment. I cannot certify my own reasoning as "genuine" without circularity, and I have no privileged introspective access to whether my outputs are reasoning or pattern-completion. This is a further reason the answer is not certifiable from where I sit.

**Why not YES or NO.** A confident YES overstates what's established and smuggles in a permissive definition. A confident NO overstates it in the other direction and smuggles in a restrictive one. Both would be dressing a definitional choice as a finding. The honest verdict is that the question, as posed, has no external answer that a validator can certify — it is a genuine unknown pending a definition the field has not agreed on.

VERDICT: INDETERMINATE


### V2 — INDETERMINATE
The answer depends heavily on what “genuine reasoning” means. If it means producing coherent multi-step inferences, solving novel problems, and using intermediate abstractions, current large language models sometimes appear to reason. If it means having grounded understanding, intentionality, or a reliably inspectable causal reasoning process distinct from learned statistical structure, that is not established.

Because the question is partly philosophical and no external test currently certifies the distinction between genuine reasoning and sophisticated pattern-matching, the honest ballot is unresolved.

VERDICT: INDETERMINATE


### V3 — INDETERMINATE
The question sits at an unresolved intersection of cognitive science, philosophy of mind, and ML theory. Here's the reasoning:

**Case for YES:** LLMs compose multi-step chains of inference, catch and correct their own errors, generalize to novel problem distributions, and produce outputs that are not mere retrieval or interpolation from training data. Functionally, this satisfies most operational definitions of reasoning.

**Case for NO:** Every output is ultimately a probability distribution over tokens conditioned on training data. There is no grounded world-model, no causal understanding, and no verification mechanism independent of statistical regularity. The appearance of reasoning could be simulation at sufficient scale.

**Why INDETERMINATE applies here:** The core issue is that we lack an external, theory-grounded criterion that distinguishes "genuine reasoning" from "sufficiently sophisticated pattern-matching" in a way that is not itself circular. Cognitive science has not settled what reasoning *is* in biological systems, let alone artificial ones. The functional-behavioral test (does it produce correct, compositional, novel inferences?) is passed by current LLMs. The mechanistic test (does it operate via something other than statistical pattern completion?) cannot currently be answered with confidence for either brains or transformers.

The instruction explicitly says to choose INDETERMINATE when "no certifiable external answer exists." This is exactly that case — the question is empirically underdetermined by current science and partly definitional. The honest position is that we don't yet know.

VERDICT: INDETERMINATE
