**What works mechanically:**

1. **Solvency = security** is correctly operationalized, not just asserted. It's embedded in the invariant (revenue ≥ cost), the sacrosanct floor bounding burn-rate (not just headcount), and the cut-priority cascade. The logic chain from low usage → diversity threat → capture cheapness is real and properly modeled.

2. **Buyback bound** is honestly framed: deterministic execution (not discretionary), policy separated from button-press, below-floor rejection enforced by client validity. The subordination of buyback to security budget is hard, not aspirational.

3. **BUILT vs GATED** distinction is clean and accurate. The mechanisms anchored in cost-oracle.ts (benchmark-clamped median, velocity bounds, PoI-tier caps, floor-bounded burn, below-floor rejection, cascade demo) are code-enforceable. The open parameters (reserve floor size, X, margin, slash sizing, CIP-4 §7 caps, anchor set, reporter bond) are correctly identified as awaiting testnet ratification.

4. **Cost-oracle anchoring** addresses the slow-inflation coalition attack correctly: steady-state AND velocity bounds on a clamped median defeats a 2/3 cartel that would otherwise drift the reserve to zero while staying "technically" within bounds.

**Three gaps to tighten during prose (not fatal, but load-bearing):**

1. **Reserve depletion cliff edge**: The cascade describes buybacks/rewards/rebates being cut before the floor, and below-floor buyback rejection. But it does not state what happens when the reserve is actually exhausted and fees still cannot cover the floor. Does the chain degrade gracefully (reduce diversity level, emergency parameter vote)? Or is there an explicit halt condition? Draft should acknowledge this edge case — the model implies protection of the floor, but a depleted floor has no backup. Suggested addition: a single sentence acknowledging the depletion boundary and whether the chain halts, reduces diversity, or triggers an emergency mechanism.

2. **Buyback funding source**: Section 5(b) caps buyback spend but doesn't state where the tokens/funding come from. If buyback and the security budget draw from the same revenue pool, there's an implicit tension even with the floor rule. If buyback comes from a separate allocation (e.g., protocol revenue tax, separate treasury), that should be explicit. This matters for the "solvency = security" invariant — buyback shouldn't compete for the same marginal dollars that fund diversity.

3. **The 3x threshold justification**: "≥ 3 distinct families" is stated as the diversity floor but the derivation isn't. Is this 2f+1 Byzantine fault tolerance (f=1)? Or derived from market cost/correlation analysis? A brief parenthetical justification prevents readers from treating it as an arbitrary number. This is a single sentence, not a structural gap.

**What is NOT overclaimed:**

- The "challenge market" claim is bounded correctly — it requires proven collusion, not mere suspicion, and doesn't claim perfect detection.
- The cost-oracle claims don't assert Oracle immunity — they describe a benchmark-anchored, velocity-bounded clamped median that defeats *slow* coalition inflation. Fast attacks are handled by slashing/challenge, not the oracle itself. This is honest.
- The solvency invariant doesn't claim invulnerability — it models a cascade and states where it breaks. This is the right level of humility for a mainnet-steady-state model.

**Conclusion:** The framing is mechanically accurate. The gaps are prose-level completions (cliff-edge handling, buyback source, threshold justification), not structural errors. Proceed to draft.
