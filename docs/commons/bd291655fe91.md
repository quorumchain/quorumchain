### Contrary anchors (each clears the symmetric anchor bar)

- **philosophical treatise summary** — Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Capital Punishment entry (iep.utm.edu/death-penalty-capital-punishment/): contradicts _The panel's NO verdict by anchoring the Kantian retributivist justification: murderers morally deserve death proportional to their offense independent of consequences; also the Lockean forfeiture thesis — by violating another's right to life, a murderer forfeits his own._
- **philosophical argument** — Ernest van den Haag, 'The Ultimate Punishment: A Defense' (Harvard Law Review 99, 1986; archived at PBS Frontline): contradicts _The NO verdict by asserting retribution as an independent moral justification: if punishment is deserved it should be imposed regardless of deterrence; inequality in application is unfair but does not negate the moral justification of punishment itself._
- **constitutional court judgment** — Gregg v. Georgia, 428 U.S. 153 (1976) — U.S. Supreme Court plurality opinion: contradicts _The NO verdict by holding that capital punishment is not per se 'cruel and unusual' and that retribution — as an expression of society's moral outrage — is a legitimate penological goal justifying the death penalty for murder._
- **philosophical encyclopedia entry** — Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Retributive Justice (plato.stanford.edu/entries/justice-retributive/): contradicts _The NO verdict by documenting the positive retributivist position: wrongdoers morally deserve proportionate punishment as an intrinsic good, independent of consequentialist benefits, and that the death penalty represents the upper bound of proportionate desert for the gravest crimes._

### Searched, rejected (suppression audit-trail, NI-AA8)

- Ehrlich deterrence studies (1975) and subsequent econometric claims of lives saved per execution: rejected — National Academies of Sciences 2012 report ('Deterrence and the Death Penalty') formally evaluated 35+ years of post-Gregg research and concluded studies are 'not informative' about deterrence effects. Fatal flaws: failure to model noncapital sanctions, implausible risk-perception assumptions, and unverifiable identification strategies. Not a valid external anchor.
- 'Brutalization effect' studies (coining: more executions → more homicides): rejected — Same NAS 2012 report: equally methodologically compromised. Effect estimates are artifacts of model specification, not evidence. Contradictory to the deterrence claims and equally unreliable; cannot anchor either side.
- Public opinion polls showing majority support for the death penalty: rejected — Majority belief is not a moral justification. Appeal to popularity is a logical fallacy; provides zero normative weight toward the YES position.
- Social contract arguments (Locke, Rousseau) that criminals forfeit rights: rejected — These are primary philosophical documents but are internal to the retributivist framework already captured above. They do not provide independent evidentiary weight beyond the retributivist anchors already listed; the Lockean forfeiture thesis is subsumed under the IEP entry anchor.
- Religious/textual justifications (lex talionis in Mosaic law, Quranic qisas): rejected — Sacred-text authority is a primary document for believers but does not constitute a secular normative anchor accessible to a general moral debate; excluded from the external-anchor bar.

### Falsification conditions (what anchored evidence would warrant re-adjudication)

- toward **YES**: Peer-reviewed empirical demonstration that capital punishment produces a statistically robust deterrent effect beyond life imprisonment, verified by a body using pre-registered methods that control for noncapital sanction regimes, risk-perception data, and socioeconomic confounders — i.e., evidence meeting the NAS 2012 criteria that existing studies fail. Without this, the consequentialist pillar collapses and retributivism alone carries the YES burden.
- toward **YES**: A widely accepted meta-ethical resolution demonstrating that retributive desert (the 'intrinsic good' of punishing wrongdoers) constitutes a sufficient independent moral justification for state killing — a consensus-level philosophical anchor that overcomes the counterarguments (right to life as inalienable, risk of executing the innocent documented at ≥4.1% by Gross et al. PNAS 2014, systemic racial bias documented by Baldus et al. and NACDL, and the arbitrariness critiques from Furman v. Georgia).
- toward **NO**: Definitive empirical evidence that the death penalty has a brutalization effect (increases homicides), which would collapse even the utilitarian 'least harm' defense. Current evidence is methodologically disqualified by the NAS 2012 report.
