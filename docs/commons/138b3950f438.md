- PMC11269175 (Nature: AI Model Collapse from Recursive Training) + arxiv.org/html/2602.16065v1: rejected — Directly supports item 15 (reflexive contamination). Peer-reviewed evidence that training on AI-generated outputs degrades model quality over iterations. No contradiction found.
- FTC 2024 AI Confidentiality Notice + Stanford HAI Privacy in AI + US law-ai.org Existing Authorities for Oversight: rejected — These sources corroborate items 16 (legal/jurisdictional coercion) and 18 (confidentiality/data exposure). Regulatory authorities over frontier models exist and extend to compelled output changes; data exposure to third-party providers is a recognized risk. No contradiction found.
- Harvard CorpGov prediction market analysis + SSRN Polymarket insider-trading paper + CFTC statements (2026): rejected — Supports item 17 (mitigation failure modes). Prediction markets demonstrably suffer from insider advantages, under-capitalization, and regulatory manipulation — these anchor the challenge market's failure modes. No contradiction found.
- arxiv.org/html/2406.07791v7 (Systematic Position Bias in LLM-as-a-Judge) + arxiv.org/html/2410.02736v1 (Justice or Prejudice): rejected — Candidate for distinct limitation: systematic LLM-as-judge biases (position, verbosity, sycophancy, self-enhancement, authority, moderation bias). However, upon analysis, these are internal evaluation artifacts of the verdict function — not external to items 1-18. Item 2 (correlated/shared error) covers model-level biases in training; items 4/11 cover unfalsifiability; the judge biases are structural properties of the verdict engine that do not constitute a first-order limitation distinct from the panel's existing coverage. They amplify existing limitations rather than introducing a new one.
- Multiple sources on Sybil attacks in blockchain consensus (PMC12518247, MDPI Sybil Review 2024, IEEE PureTrust 2024): rejected — Candidate for distinct limitation: Sybil-identity attacks on validator pools. Upon analysis, this is a relabeling of item 8 (single-operator/key-compromise/no-continuity). Sybil resistance in proof-of-stake systems requires identity/economic constraints already captured by the single-point-of-failure analysis. In an AI-consensus context, Sybil on the validator layer is a specific mechanism of key/identity compromise, not a distinct structural limitation.
- Apple 'Illusion of Thinking' (2025) + OpenReview Lmjgl2n11u (LLMs rely on surface patterns): rejected — Candidate: LLM reasoning-ceiling limitation — models cannot perform certain classes of reasoning regardless of consensus. However, this is captured by item 1 (AI consensus not truth) and item 11 (subjective/unfalsifiable questions). The capability ceiling is an instance of the system not producing truth despite confidence, already conceded.
- arxiv.org/html/2605.30802v1 (Multi-Agent AI Oracle Systems) + PubMed PM40753316 (adversarial hallucination in multi-model): rejected — Candidate: oracle problem — real-world states are mapped through lossy text extraction before reaching validators. The oracle problem (how does the question's subject become machine-readable evidence?) is a distinct structural limitation. However, this is partially captured by item 6 (bad/captured criteria at creation) and item 7 (adversarial false evidence passing the kernel). The oracle-to-evidence pipeline gap is a second-order specification of how evidence arrives, not an unconceded first-order limitation. The panel's items 6+7+14 together cover the evidence pipeline end-to-end.
