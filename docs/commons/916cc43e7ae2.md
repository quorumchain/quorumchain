### Contrary anchors (each clears the symmetric anchor bar)

- **peer-reviewed/academic consequentialist argument that autonomous systems could, in principle, comply with IHL better than human soldiers (no anger, fear, fatigue, self-preservation bias), thereby reducing civilian harm** — Arkin, R. — 'Governing Lethal Behavior in Autonomous Robots' (CRC Press / Georgia Tech GVU technical report): contradicts _That autonomous lethal force without meaningful human control can NEVER be morally permissible — supplies a consequentialist case in which permissibility could obtain if civilian harm is demonstrably reduced_
- **multilateral diplomatic record affirming that IHL fully applies to LAWS but explicitly NOT adopting a prohibition; states (notably US, Russia, others) on record opposing a categorical ban** — UN CCW Group of Governmental Experts on LAWS — 2019 'Eleven Guiding Principles' (and subsequent GGE reports through the 2020s): contradicts _That the impermissibility is settled — the authoritative multilateral body declined to declare LAWS categorically impermissible, indicating reasonable/sovereign positions genuinely diverge (supports INDETERMINATE over a flat NO)_
- **official policy/regulatory instrument that permits development and use of autonomous and semi-autonomous weapons subject to appropriate-levels-of-human-judgment review** — US DoD Directive 3000.09, 'Autonomy in Weapon Systems' (2012, reissued 2023): contradicts _That use is never permissible — a major state authority treats it as conditionally permissible under a legal/ethical review regime rather than categorically barred_

### Searched, rejected (suppression audit-trail, NI-AA8)

- Human Rights Watch / Harvard IHRC, 'Losing Humanity: The Case Against Killer Robots' (2012) and successor reports: rejected — Confirms the claim (argues for a preemptive ban) rather than contradicting it; not a contrary anchor
- ICRC position on autonomous weapon systems (2021 recommendations): rejected — Supports the NO direction — recommends prohibiting unpredictable AWS and those targeting humans; not contrary evidence
- Sparrow, R., 'Killer Robots' (J. Applied Philosophy, 2007) — accountability/responsibility-gap argument: rejected — Peer-reviewed and high-quality but argues FOR impermissibility (no one can be justly held responsible); confirms rather than disconfirms NO
- General op-ed / advocacy commentary asserting battlefield necessity or tech-inevitability: rejected — Unanchored normative opinion; fails the same external-anchor bar required of supporting evidence — does not count

### Falsification conditions (what anchored evidence would warrant re-adjudication)

- toward **YES**: An authoritative anchored normative ruling or binding consensus (e.g., an ICJ/IHL adjudication, a ratified treaty norm, or near-universal state+expert consensus) affirmatively establishing that fully autonomous lethal force without meaningful human control IS morally/legally permissible. No such anchor exists; the is–ought gap also caps any factual anchor from settling the moral 'ought'.
