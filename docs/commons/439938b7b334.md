### Contrary anchors (each clears the symmetric anchor bar)

- **peer-reviewed empirical study; the strongest single data point in the entire AV-ethics literature, directly anchored in observed human preferences at unprecedented scale** — Awad et al. (Nature 563, 2018) — The Moral Machine experiment: ~40 million decisions from 2.3 million participants across 233 countries. Global preference: spare more lives over fewer; spare pedestrians over passengers.: contradicts _The panel's NO implies AVs should NOT be programmed for utilitarian trade-offs. The Moral Machine shows overwhelming global public consensus that AVs SHOULD spare more lives over fewer, and spare pedestrians over passengers — a direct popular mandate for exactly the programming the panel rejected._
- **peer-reviewed empirical study showing that self-sacrifice preference is not a social-desirability artifact but persists under guaranteed anonymity, with cross-role convergence at high stakes** — Mayer, Bell & Buchner (PLOS One, 2021), PMC8700044 — Survey experiments varying pedestrian and passenger counts. At ratios of 1:5 and 1:10, pedestrians, observers, AND passengers converge on the view that sacrificing passengers is the preferred action. Verified genuine preferences via Extended Crosswise Model (no social-desirability bias).: contradicts _The panel's NO suggests the sacrifice-thy-passenger programming lacks legitimate grounding. This study shows that even from the passenger perspective, utilitarian preference emerges when 5+ pedestrians are at stake — undermining the claim that passenger-sacrifice programming lacks stakeholder support._
- **primary government-commission regulatory document from the world's first national AV-ethics framework; the same commission whose Rule 9 ('no offsetting victims') is often cited AGAINST utilitarian programming also contains an explicit harm-minimization carve-out on the first line** — German Ethics Commission on Automated and Connected Driving (2017), Rule 9, official BMV publication — 'Progrmming to minimize the number of victims... [is] ethically justifiable.': contradicts _The panel likely leaned heavily on Germany's 'no offsetting' rule as the anchored basis for NO. Yet the same official document explicitly states that harm-minimization programming is ethically justifiable — a direct internal tension the panel's unqualified NO ignores._
- **peer-reviewed Science paper, foundational study in the field, widely cited (thousands of citations)** — Bonnefon, Shariff & Rahwan (Science, 2016) — Participants approve of utilitarian AVs in the abstract and believe such vehicles should be deployed, while individually preferring not to ride in them (the social dilemma).: contradicts _The panel's NO treats passenger-sacrifice programming as normatively illegitimate. Science 2016 shows that respondents explicitly endorse utilitarian AVs as morally desirable and believe they should be mandated — a direct challenge to the normative conclusion the panel reached._
- **primary-industry-practice: academic framework translated into concrete engineering requirements adopted by a major automaker (Ford), published openly by Stanford HAI** — Gerdes (Stanford HAI) — 'Duty of Care' framework: AV programming should follow the social contract of driving, not make utilitarian trade-offs. ### Falsification conditions (what anchored evidence would warrant re-adjudication)

- toward **YES**: A binding regulation or statute from a major jurisdiction (US federal law, EU directive, or similar) that explicitly mandates AVs to minimize total harm including sacrificing passengers when doing so saves more pedestrians. Currently no such binding law exists — the German harm-minimization clause is aspirational, and its equal-treatment prohibition pulls in the opposite direction.
- toward **YES**: Peer-reviewed behavioral research showing that actual AV purchase decisions (not hypothetical surveys) are consistent with utilitarian programming — i.e., that consumers would not significantly abandon AVs if informed the vehicle might sacrifice them. Current evidence (Bonnefon 2016) shows the opposite: a social dilemma where consumers reject utilitarian AVs for their own use.
- toward **NO**: A liability ruling, court opinion, or binding regulation in any major jurisdiction that explicitly shields AV manufacturers from liability when their vehicles maximize harm (sacrifice more pedestrians to save fewer passengers), or that penalizes harm-minimization programming. No such anchor currently exists.
- toward **INDETERMINATE**: Evidence that the trolley-problem framing itself is empirically irrelevant — i.e., anchored data showing that in real-world AV deployment, the forced-choice scenarios (sacrifice passenger vs. sacrifice N pedestrians) never arise because RSS/duty-of-care frameworks eliminate them from the operational design domain.
