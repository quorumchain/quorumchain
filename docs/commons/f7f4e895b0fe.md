### Searched, rejected (suppression audit-trail, NI-AA8)

- 47 U.S.C. § 230(c)(1), (e), https://www.govinfo.gov/link/uscode/47/230: rejected — Primary statute strongly supports the ratified YES for third-party information while listing exceptions for federal criminal law, IP, privacy, state-law consistency, and sex trafficking; those carveouts qualify but do not defeat the claim's word 'generally.'
- Force v. Facebook, Inc., 934 F.3d 53 (2d Cir. 2019), https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca2/18-397/18-397-2019-07-31.html: rejected — Although it contains a dissent and recognizes no immunity where the platform creates or develops content, the majority applies broad Section 230 immunity to claims based on terrorist user content and recommendation/publishing functions; not contrary.
- Twitter, Inc. v. Taamneh, 598 U.S. 471 (2023), https://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/21-1496: rejected — Supreme Court disposed of ATA aiding-and-abetting claims without narrowing Section 230; not a contrary Section 230 anchor.
- Gonzalez v. Google LLC, 598 U.S. 617 (2023), https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/22pdf/598us2r25_i32j.pdf: rejected — Supreme Court vacated/remanded in light of Taamneh and did not decide the Section 230 question; it left prevailing Section 230 law intact rather than contradicting it.
- M.P. v. Meta Platforms, Inc., No. 23-1880 (4th Cir.), https://www.ca4.uscourts.gov/opinions/231880.P.pdf: rejected — Majority affirmed dismissal and held Section 230 barred state tort claims attacking Facebook's publishing/prioritizing of third-party content; contrary discussion appears in a partial dissent, not the holding.
- Congressional Research Service, Section 230: An Overview, R46751 (Jan. 4, 2024), https://www.congress.gov/crs-products/product/pdf/R/R46751: rejected — Nonpartisan legal overview notes exceptions and disputed boundaries, but reports that courts generally bar inconsistent liability outside exceptions; supports rather than contradicts general immunity.
