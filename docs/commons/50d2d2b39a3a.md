### Contrary anchors (each clears the symmetric anchor bar)

- **international-human-rights-report** — UN Human Rights Council, A/HRC/47/25, Report of the Special Rapporteur on disinformation and freedom of opinion and expression, paras. 70-72: https://documents.un.org/access.nsf/get?DS=A/HRC/47/25&Lang=E&OpenAgent=: contradicts _Contradicts a broad YES by anchoring that misinformation rules are often vague, automated/context-poor enforcement increases removal of permissible content, and remedies are inadequate._
- **platform-oversight-adjudication** — Meta Oversight Board, Claimed COVID-19 cure decision, 2021: https://www.oversightboard.com/decision/fb-xwjqbu9a/: contradicts _Contradicts categorical justification for removal by finding a real COVID-misinformation removal failed Meta rules, values, and international human-rights standards, and that less intrusive tools were not shown inadequate._
- **empirical-platform-study** — Broniatowski et al., The efficacy of Facebook's vaccine misinformation policies and architecture during the COVID-19 pandemic, Science Advances, 2023, DOI 10.1126/sciadv.adh2132: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adh2132: contradicts _Contradicts the public-discourse-protection rationale by finding Facebook vaccine-misinformation removal reduced anti-vaccine posts but did not durably reduce engagement and produced collateral/perverse effects, including removal of pro-vaccine content and more misinformative remaining content._
- **survey-experiment** — Appel, Pan & Roberts, Partisan conflict over content moderation is more than disagreement about facts, Science Advances, 2023: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10624338/: contradicts _Contradicts the assumption that lawful misinformation removal straightforwardly protects shared public discourse by anchoring that even when falsity is recognized, removal is itself politically contested and perceived as censorship by substantial partisan groups._
- **civil-society-standard** — Santa Clara Principles on Transparency and Accountability in Content Moderation, 2021 iteration: https://santaclaraprinciples.org/: contradicts _Contradicts unconstrained YES by anchoring that moderation actions, including removal/downranking, require human-rights review, due process, clarity, state-request disclosure, accuracy, and appeals to be rights-respecting._

### Searched, rejected (suppression audit-trail, NI-AA8)

- Murthy v. Missouri, U.S. Supreme Court, 2024: https://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/23-411_2024-06-26: rejected — Relevant to government jawboning and standing, but the majority did not resolve whether private platform removal of lawful misinformation is morally justified or unjustified.
- Budak et al., Misunderstanding the harms of online misinformation, Nature, 2024: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07417-w: rejected — Peer-reviewed and relevant to overstated empirical harm claims, but it does not directly show that platform removal is unjustified; it mainly narrows factual premises for intervention.
- Harris, Social Evidence Tampering and the Epistemology of Content Moderation, Topoi, 2024: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11245-024-10101-3: rejected — Useful philosophical critique of epistemic costs, but less externally decisive because it is primarily conceptual rather than empirical, legal, or adjudicative.
- Meta Oversight Board, Removal of COVID-19 misinformation policy advisory opinion, 2023: https://www.oversightboard.com/decision/pao-sabu4p2s/: rejected — Mixed anchor: it recognizes freedom-expression burdens but also accepts continued removal in some emergency contexts, so it does not cleanly disconfirm the ratified YES.
- EFF / civil-society commentary on content moderation and Santa Clara Principles: rejected — Valuable rights analysis, but advocacy commentary was rejected where the underlying Santa Clara Principles supplied the stronger primary anchor.

### Falsification conditions (what anchored evidence would warrant re-adjudication)

- toward **NO**: Robust cross-platform empirical evidence that removing lawful misinformation predictably worsens public discourse, produces more false belief or polarization than non-removal alternatives, and cannot be corrected by due process, transparency, or narrower standards.
- toward **NO**: Authoritative legal or human-rights determinations establishing that removal of lawful misinformation by dominant platforms is generally incompatible with freedom of expression even when done transparently and without state coercion.
- toward **YES**: High-quality causal evidence that removal of lawful misinformation materially improves discourse-level outcomes compared with labels, downranking, counterspeech, and user controls, while wrongful-removal and viewpoint-bias harms remain low and remediable.
- toward **INDETERMINATE**: A balanced body of external evidence showing large, persistent, and domain-dependent tradeoffs where neither removal nor non-removal reliably protects public discourse across health, election, war, and civic contexts.
